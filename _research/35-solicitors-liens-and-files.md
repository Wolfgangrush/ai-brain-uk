# Solicitors' Liens and File Ownership — UK

- [Solicitors Act 1974, Section 68](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1974/47/section/68)

## Last Verified
2026-05-18

## Authority Tier
Bar / Regulator Rule / Statute

## Currency Warning
Recent High Court rulings (e.g., *Ellis v John Hodge Solicitors [2022]*) have clarified that a lien cannot be used to block disclosure in professional negligence claims against the solicitor.

## Key Facts
1. **The Retaining Lien:** A common law right for a solicitor to keep a client's papers and property until all fees and disbursements are paid.
2. **Ownership (Client):** Documents belonging to the client include:
    - Original deeds, passports, and documents sent to the firm.
    - Final versions of letters and documents created as the client's agent.
3. **Ownership (Solicitor):** Documents belonging to the solicitor include:
    - Internal file notes and drafts.
    - Copies of outgoing letters.
    - Time sheets and accounting records.
4. **Exceptions (Material Prejudice):** The SRA advises that a lien should not be enforced if it would cause "material prejudice" to the client's position in an ongoing matter.
5. **GDPR Intersection:** A lien cannot be used to refuse a **Subject Access Request (SAR)** for personal data, though the solicitor may provide the data in a non-original format.
6. **Court Power (s.68):** The court can order a solicitor to hand over a file regardless of a lien, often requiring the client to pay the disputed sum into court as security.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The solicitor has a common law right to retain client papers... as security for the payment of the solicitor's costs." — *Law Society Guidance (Paraphrased)*

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
A solo advocate should use a lien as a "last resort." If a client fails to pay, the soloist must distinguish between the "client's property" (which they can hold) and their "own property" (which the client has no right to anyway). If the client instructs a new solicitor, the soloist should usually hand over the file on an "undertaking" that the new firm will pay the fees from any eventually recovered costs, rather than risking a "material prejudice" complaint to the SRA.
