## Currency Warning
The OAPA 1861 is notoriously archaic and has been the subject of numerous Law Commission reform proposals, but it remains the active law for non-fatal offences. The Fraud Act 2006 is modern and broad.

## Key Facts
1. **Theft (s.1 Theft Act 1968):** Dishonest appropriation of property belonging to another with the intention of permanently depriving the other of it.
2. **Robbery (s.8 Theft Act 1968):** Stealing while using or threatening force immediately before or at the time of doing so.
3. **Fraud (ss.2–4 Fraud Act 2006):** Can be committed in three ways:
    - By false representation.
    - By failing to disclose information (where there is a legal duty).
    - By abuse of position.
    - *All require dishonesty and intent to make a gain or cause a loss.*
4. **GBH with Intent (s.18 OAPA 1861):** Unlawfully wounding or causing grievous bodily harm with specific intent to do serious harm or resist arrest. (Max: Life).
5. **Wounding/Inflicting GBH (s.20 OAPA 1861):** Unlawfully and maliciously wounding or inflicting serious harm. Requires proof the defendant foresaw *some* harm. (Max: 5 years).
6. **ABH (s.47 OAPA 1861):** Assault or battery causing actual bodily harm (e.g., bruising, psychological injury). (Max: 5 years).
7. **Common Assault/Battery:** Now largely governed by the **Criminal Justice Act 1988 (s.39)** as summary offences (Max: 6 months).

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"A person is guilty of theft if he dishonestly appropriates property belonging to another with the intention of permanently depriving the other of it." — *Theft Act 1968, s.1(1)*

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
In criminal defense, the distinction between **s.18 and s.20 OAPA** is critical for plea negotiations and sentencing. A solo advocate must meticulously analyze the *mens rea* (intent vs recklessness). For fraud cases, the broadness of the 2006 Act means the focus is almost entirely on proving or disproving **dishonesty** (using the *Ivey/Barton* test). Understanding the specific "gateways" for bad character and hearsay under the CJA 2003 is essential for a robust defense.
