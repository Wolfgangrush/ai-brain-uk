## Currency Warning
The duty of confidentiality is lifelong and survives the death of the client. It must be balanced against the increasingly strict **Anti-Money Laundering (AML)** disclosure requirements which can mandate "tipping off" prohibitions.

## Key Facts
1. **Duty of Confidentiality (SRA 6.3 / BSB CD6):** An absolute duty to keep the affairs of current and former clients confidential. Disclosure is only permitted if required by law or with informed client consent.
2. **Duty of Disclosure (SRA 6.4):** A solicitor must disclose to the client all information "material to their matter" of which the solicitor has knowledge.
3. **The Conflict of Duties (SRA 6.5):** If your duty of confidentiality to Client A conflicts with your duty of disclosure to Client B, you **cannot act** for Client B.
4. **Legal Professional Privilege (LPP):** A higher legal protection than the regulatory duty of confidentiality. LPP belongs to the client and cannot be overridden by regulators or the police without specific statutory authority or client waiver.
5. **Misleading the Court (BSB rC3):** The duty to the court is primary. You must not knowingly mislead the court, but this does not require you to breach confidentiality (e.g., if a client confesses guilt, you cannot tell the court, but you may have to withdraw if they intend to lie).
6. **Data Protection Intersection:** Compliance with the DPA 2018/UK GDPR is a subset of the wider duty of confidentiality.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"You keep the affairs of current and former clients confidential unless disclosure is required or permitted by law or the client consents." — *SRA Code of Conduct, Para 6.3*

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
A solo advocate is the "single point of failure" for confidentiality. They must ensure that their digital tools (email, cloud storage, case management) are encrypted and secure. When working in public (e.g., on a train or in a cafe), they must use privacy screens and avoid taking calls where client details can be overheard. The solo practitioner must also be aware that "material information" they learn about a former client might prevent them from taking a new, unrelated case against that client.
