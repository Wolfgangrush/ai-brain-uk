## Currency Warning
The "Freelance Solicitor" model was introduced in 2019. It is a major shift from the traditional "Recognised Sole Practice" and is still being "bedded in" by insurance providers.

## Key Facts
1. **Freelance Solicitor (Individual):** A solicitor practicing on their own account without being authorized as a firm.
    - **Pros:** No firm authorization fee, lower compliance overhead (no COLP/COFA).
    - **Cons:** Cannot employ staff, cannot hold client money, cannot use a trading name (must use personal name).
    - **Reserved Activities:** Can do litigation/probate if 3+ years PQE.
2. **Recognised Sole Practice (RSP):** An authorized firm where one solicitor is the sole principal.
    - **Pros:** Can employ staff, can use a trading name (e.g., "Smith & Co"), can hold client money.
    - **Cons:** **Unlimited personal liability** for the solicitor.
3. **Limited Company (Recognised Body):** An authorized firm incorporated as a company.
    - **Pros:** **Limited liability** for the owner(s). Often more tax-efficient.
    - **Cons:** Statutory filing requirements at Companies House.
4. **Limited Liability Partnership (LLP):** An authorized firm incorporated as an LLP.
    - **Pros:** Limited liability. Flexible partnership structure.
    - **Cons:** Requires at least two "members" (though one can sometimes be a service company).
5. **Professional Indemnity Insurance (PII):** Authorized firms must meet the SRA's "Minimum Terms and Conditions" (£2m/£3m cover). Freelancers need "adequate" cover, which may be cheaper but harder to find.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"An individual solicitor may practice as a freelance solicitor... provided they do not employ any person in connection with the provision of legal services." — *SRA Authorisation of Individuals Regulations, Reg 10.2 (paraphrased)*

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
The **Limited Company** is the "gold standard" for a solo advocate who wants to scale or protect personal assets. The **Freelance** model is best for "pure" solo practitioners (e.g., consultants) who don't need a brand or staff. A solo practitioner must appoint themselves as the COLP (Compliance Officer for Legal Practice) and COFA (Compliance Officer for Finance and Administration) in any authorized firm structure.
