## Currency Warning

## Key Facts
1. **Civil Hearsay (CEA 1995):** Hearsay is generally admissible in civil proceedings (s.1). Notice must be given, and the court decides the "weight" based on factors like the reliability of the source (s.4).
2. **Criminal Hearsay (CJA 2003):** Generally inadmissible unless it falls under statutory gateways: witness unavailable (s.116), business documents (s.117), or in the "interests of justice" (s.114).
3. **Criminal Bad Character (CJA 2003):** Previous convictions or "reprehensible behavior" are admissible only through specific "gateways," such as to correct a false impression or if relevant to an "important matter in issue" between prosecution and defense.
4. **Exclusion of Evidence (PACE s.78):** A court can exclude prosecution evidence if its admission would have an "adverse effect on the fairness of the proceedings." This is a key tool for defense advocates.
5. **Confessions (PACE s.76):** Must be excluded if obtained by "oppression" or in circumstances likely to make the confession unreliable.
6. **Business Records (CEA 1995 s.9):** Records of a business or public authority are admissible in civil cases without calling the creator, if certified.
7. **Expert Evidence (CPR Part 35 / CrimPR Part 19):** Duty is to the *court*, not the party paying the expert. Permission is required to call an expert.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"In civil proceedings evidence shall not be excluded on the ground that it is hearsay." — *Civil Evidence Act 1995, s.1(1)*

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
In civil cases, the solo advocate focuses on the **weight** of evidence—ensuring that hearsay is supported by other documents. In criminal cases, the advocate must be a "gatekeeper," proactively filing applications to exclude "bad character" evidence or challenging the admissibility of confessions under PACE s.76 and s.78. Effective use of disclosure (CPIA 1996) is essential for finding evidence that undermines the prosecution or supports the defense.
