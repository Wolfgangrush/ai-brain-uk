## Key Facts
1. **Rule C119:** Self-employed barristers can take instructions directly from the public if they follow the Public Access Rules.
2. **Mandatory Training:** A barrister must complete a BSB-approved training course before accepting direct instructions.
3. **Junior Barristers (<3 years):** Can do Public Access only if they have a "qualified person" (a barrister of 6+ years standing) available to provide guidance.
4. **Litigation Prohibition:** Public Access barristers **cannot conduct litigation** (e.g., file court forms, act as address for service) unless they have a specific "extension to their practicing certificate."
5. **Best Interests (Rule C120.3):** A barrister must advise the client to instruct a solicitor if it is in the client's best interests (e.g., if the case is too administratively complex).
6. **No Cab Rank Rule:** The "Cab Rank Rule" does not apply to Public Access; the barrister can choose whether to accept the case.
7. **Transparency:** Barristers must publish their fees and service descriptions on their websites (Section D6).

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"You must not accept instructions under the Public Access Rules if you consider that it would be in the best interests of the client... to instruct a solicitor." — *BSB Rule C120.3*

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
For a solo barrister, Public Access is the primary route for building a solo practice. However, without a "litigation extension," the barrister cannot handle the paperwork side of a case, placing a heavy burden on the lay client. Most solo barristers should apply for **litigation authorization** to provide a "full service" comparable to a solo solicitor. Junior soloists must ensure they have a formal arrangement with a senior "qualified person" to avoid a BSB breach.
