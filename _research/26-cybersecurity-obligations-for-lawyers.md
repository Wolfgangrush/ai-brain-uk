## Currency Warning
Since **1 October 2025**, holding a **Cyber Essentials** certification is mandatory for any firm or solo practitioner holding a **Criminal Legal Aid** contract with the LAA.

## Key Facts
1. **Duty of Confidentiality:** Protecting client data from cyber-attack is a core part of the lifelong duty of confidentiality (SRA 6.3 / BSB CD6).
2. **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):** Both the SRA and Bar Council now consider MFA **mandatory** for all remote access and cloud-based systems (email, case management).
3. **Encryption:** Full-disk encryption (e.g., BitLocker or FileVault) must be used on all portable devices (laptops, phones) used for client work.
4. **Breach Reporting:** Under UK GDPR, a data breach must be reported to the ICO within 72 hours if it poses a risk to individuals. Serious breaches must also be reported to the SRA/BSB.
5. **Cyber Essentials (Five Controls):**
    - Firewalls.
    - Secure Configuration.
    - User Access Control.
    - Malware Protection.
    - Patch Management (Updates within 14 days).
6. **"Bring Your Own Device" (BYOD):** Solo advocates using personal devices for work must ensure they are segregated from personal data and meet firm security standards.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"It is a barrister’s personal responsibility to ensure that they have appropriate security measures in place to protect client information." — *Bar Council Guidance*

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
A solo advocate is the "Chief Information Security Officer" of their own practice. They must move away from "consumer-grade" tools (e.g., free Gmail/Dropbox) and use professional, encrypted services. Achieving **Cyber Essentials** is the best defense against a regulatory investigation if a breach occurs, as it demonstrates the advocate took "reasonable steps" to secure their practice. The soloist should also maintain an "offline" backup to mitigate the risk of ransomware.
