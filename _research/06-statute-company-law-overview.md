# Company Law Overview — UK

## Source URL
- [Companies Act 2006 (legislation.gov.uk)](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2006/46/contents)

## Last Verified
2026-05-18

## Authority Tier
Statute

## Currency Warning
The Act is the primary framework, but it is frequently amended by secondary legislation (Statutory Instruments) regarding filing requirements and audit thresholds. The **Economic Crime and Corporate Transparency Act 2023** introduced significant changes to Companies House powers and identity verification for directors.

## Key Facts
1. **Company Formation (s.7):** A company is formed by one or more persons subscribing their names to a memorandum of association and complying with registration requirements.
2. **The Constitution (s.17):** Comprises the Articles of Association and any resolutions/agreements affecting the constitution. The Articles are a statutory contract between the company and its members.
3. **Separate Legal Personality:** A company is a legal person separate from its members (the *Salomon* principle, codified in s.16).
4. **Directors' Duties (ss.171–177):** Codified for the first time in 2006:
    - s.171: Act within powers.
    - s.172: **Promote the success of the company** (the core duty).
    - s.174: Exercise reasonable care, skill, and diligence.
    - s.175: Avoid conflicts of interest.
5. **Shareholders' Rights:** Power to appoint/remove directors (s.168) and approve major transactions. **Ordinary Resolutions** require >50% and **Special Resolutions** require >=75%.
6. **Derivative Claims (s.260):** A mechanism for shareholders to sue directors in the name of the company for negligence or breach of duty.
7. **Filings:** Mandatory annual filing of accounts and a "confirmation statement" to Companies House.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"A director of a company must act in the way he considers, in good faith, would be most likely to promote the success of the company for the benefit of its members as a whole." — *Companies Act 2006, s.172(1)*

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
A solo advocate advising small businesses must emphasize the **s.172 duty** and the importance of **shareholder agreements** to prevent deadlock in quasi-partnerships. For solo practitioners who incorporate their own firm, they must strictly separate personal and company finances to avoid "piercing the corporate veil" and ensure all statutory filings are made to avoid strike-off or personal prosecution of the director.
