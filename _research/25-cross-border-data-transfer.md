## Currency Warning
The UK **International Data Transfer Agreement (IDTA)** and the **Addendum to the EU Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)** are the mandatory frameworks for transfers from the UK to "non-adequate" countries since 2022.

## Key Facts
1. **Adequacy Decisions:** Data can flow freely to countries with a UK "adequacy decision" (includes the EU/EEA, Canada, Japan, etc.).
2. **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs):** If no adequacy exists (e.g., most of the USA, India, China), you must use the UK **IDTA** or the **UK Addendum** to the EU SCCs.
3. **The "Data Transfer Risk Assessment" (TRA):** Before transferring data to a non-adequate country, you **must** conduct a TRA to ensure the data will be protected in practice (taking into account the foreign country's surveillance laws).
4. **The "Data Privacy Framework" (DPF):** The UK-US Data Bridge (2023) allows data to flow to US organizations that have "self-certified" under the DPF without an IDTA.
5. **Explicit Consent:** A transfer can take place if the data subject (the client) has explicitly consented after being informed of the risks.
6. **Public Interest Exception:** Transfers necessary for "important reasons of public interest" or legal claims.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The IDTA and the Addendum are designed to ensure that when personal data is transferred abroad, it continues to benefit from a high level of protection." — *ICO Guidance*

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
If a solo advocate uses a US-based SaaS tool (e.g., an AI research tool or a specific legal CRM), they must check if the provider is on the **UK-US Data Bridge** list. If not, the advocate must sign an **IDTA** with the provider and complete a **TRA**. For soloists, using EU/UK-based software is significantly lower-risk and reduces the administrative burden of international compliance.
