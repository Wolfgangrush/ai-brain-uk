## Currency Warning
The Law Commission recently completed a review of the **Arbitration Act 1996** (2023/24), resulting in the **Arbitration Act 2024** (commencing in phases) which clarifies the law on governing law and the powers of the court to support arbitral proceedings.

## Key Facts
1. **Arbitration Act 1996:** Provides the legal framework for arbitration in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland. It emphasizes party autonomy and limited court intervention.
2. **The Arbitral Award:** An award is final and binding on the parties and can be enforced in the same way as a court judgment (s.66).
3. **Court Intervention (s.9):** If a party brings a court claim in breach of an arbitration agreement, the court **must** stay the proceedings unless the agreement is null and void.
4. **Mediation (ADR):** A voluntary, confidential process where a neutral third party helps the parties reach a settlement.
5. **Compulsory Mediation?** Since the 2024 ruling in *Churchill v Merthyr Tydfil*, courts **can** order parties to engage in ADR if it is proportionate and does not impair their right to a fair trial.
6. **Costs Sanctions (Halsey/Churchill):** Unreasonably refusing to engage in ADR can result in significant costs penalties, even for the winning party.
7. **Mediation Privilege:** Communications during mediation are generally "without prejudice" and cannot be disclosed in court.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The object of arbitration is to obtain the fair resolution of disputes by an impartial tribunal without unnecessary delay or expense." — *Arbitration Act 1996, s.1(a)*

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
A solo advocate must advise every civil litigation client on the merits of **ADR** at the earliest stage. Failure to do so is a breach of the **Pre-Action Protocols**. In commercial contracts, the advocate must check for **Arbitration Clauses** before issuing a court claim, as an "oops" filing in court could lead to a s.9 stay and a costs order against their client. For soloists, mediation is often the most cost-effective way to resolve a dispute without the risk of an "all-or-nothing" trial.
