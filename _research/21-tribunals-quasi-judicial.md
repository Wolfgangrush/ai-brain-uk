## Currency Warning
The tribunal system is frequently updated with new "chambers" for specialized areas. The **Employment Tribunal** remains outside the main TCEA 2007 structure but shares many procedural similarities.

## Key Facts
1. **Two-Tier Structure (TCEA 2007):**
    - **First-tier Tribunal (FTT):** Hears the initial appeal or claim.
    - **Upper Tribunal (UT):** Hears appeals on points of law from the FTT. Equivalent in status to the High Court.
2. **Chambers:** The FTT is divided into specialized chambers:
    - Social Entitlement (Benefits).
    - Health, Education and Social Care (Mental Health, SEND).
    - War Pensions and Armed Forces Compensation.
    - Tax Chamber.
    - General Regulatory Chamber (Charities, Information Rights).
    - Immigration and Asylum Chamber.
    - Property Chamber (Landlord/Tenant, Land Registration).
3. **Procedural Rules:** Each chamber has its own set of **Tribunal Procedure Rules** (e.g., The Tribunal Procedure (First-tier Tribunal) (Property Chamber) Rules 2013).
4. **Costs:** Unlike civil courts, most tribunals are "no-costs" jurisdictions, meaning each party pays their own legal fees regardless of the outcome (subject to "unreasonable conduct" exceptions).
5. **Informality:** Tribunals are designed to be more accessible than courts, but legally represented parties are still expected to adhere to strict directions and evidence rules.
6. **Employment Tribunals:** Governed by the **Employment Tribunals Act 1996** and the **Employment Tribunals Rules of Procedure 2013**.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"The First-tier Tribunal is to consist of... such chambers as the Lord Chancellor may by order specify." — *TCEA 2007, s.3(1) / s.7(1) (Paraphrased)*

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
Tribunal work is a mainstay for many solo practitioners, particularly in **Employment, Immigration, and Property**. The "no-costs" rule means advocates must often work on fixed fees or specialized "Legal Help" (Legal Aid) rates. A solo advocate must be a specialist in the specific **Procedure Rules** of the relevant chamber, as they differ significantly from the CPR. The Upper Tribunal is a formal superior court of record; appeals there require high-level legal argument on "error of law."
