## Currency Warning
The Protocol is a stable industry agreement (since 2003) endorsed by all major legal bodies. It was recently reaffirmed by the Attorney General's Pro Bono Committee in 2024.

## Key Facts
1. **Definition:** Pro bono legal work is legal advice or representation provided by lawyers to individuals/groups who cannot afford to pay and where public funding is unavailable.
2. **Strictly Free:** Work must be provided for free, without expectation of payment regardless of the outcome.
3. **Same Standard of Care:** Pro bono work must be given the **same priority and attention** as paid work.
4. **Competence:** A lawyer should only undertake pro bono work in areas where they are competent and adequately supervised.
5. **Adverse Costs:** Clients must be warned that they may still be liable for the *other side's* costs if they lose.
6. **Pro Bono Costs Orders (s.194):** If a pro bono client wins, the court can order the losing side to pay "pro bono costs" to the Access to Justice Foundation (under s.194 Legal Services Act 2007).
7. **Insurance:** Practitioners must ensure their Professional Indemnity Insurance covers pro bono activities.

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"Pro bono legal work is provided voluntarily and free of charge to those who cannot afford to pay for it and for whom no public funding is available." — *National Pro Bono Protocol*

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
A solo advocate should allocate a small percentage of their time to pro bono work to build experience and professional reputation. Using the **Advocate** platform (for barristers) or **LawWorks** (for solicitors) streamlines the process of finding vetted cases. The soloist must be careful to treat pro bono files with the same administrative rigor as paid files, including conflict checks and formal engagement letters that explain the risk of adverse costs.
