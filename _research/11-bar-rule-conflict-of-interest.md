## Currency Warning
The SRA Standards and Regulations (2019) simplified the conflict rules but placed more emphasis on the professional judgment of the individual solicitor. There are no longer "prescriptive" lists of allowed scenarios beyond the two main exceptions.

## Key Facts
1. **Own Interest Conflict (SRA 6.1 / BSB rC21.1):** Absolute prohibition on acting if there is a conflict (or significant risk) between the practitioner's own interests and the client's interests. This cannot be waived by consent.
2. **Client Conflict (SRA 6.2):** You must not act for two or more clients in the same or related matters if there is a conflict, **unless**:
    - They have a **substantially common interest**, OR
    - They are **competing for the same objective**.
3. **Informed Consent:** Even if an exception applies, you must obtain **informed consent in writing** from all clients, and it must be reasonable to act for all.
4. **Information Barriers:** While larger firms use "Chinese Walls," these are generally **not possible** for a solo practitioner. If a solo advocate has confidential information for Client A that is material to Client B, they generally cannot act for Client B.
5. **Barrister Independence (BSB rC15):** A barrister may only act for multiple clients if they can act in the best interests of each as if they were the "only" client.
6. **The "Cab Rank Rule" (BSB rC29):** Self-employed barristers must accept any instructions within their expertise and availability, regardless of the identity of the client or the nature of the case (subject to specific exceptions like conflict).

## Verbatim Quote (if applicable)
"You do not act if there is an own interest conflict or a significant risk of such a conflict." — *SRA Code of Conduct, Para 6.1*

## Practical Implication for Solo Advocate
For a solo practitioner, conflict checking is a "binary" task. Without a team to build information barriers, most conflicts are insurmountable. A solo advocate must have a robust **Conflict Check** software system that searches all current and former client names before taking a new instruction. Documentation of the rationale for accepting a "substantially common interest" case is vital for regulatory defense.
